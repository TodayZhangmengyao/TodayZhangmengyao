Field Experiments

Design, analysis, and exemplary studies — from classic RCTs to platform experiments.

Field experiments are the empirical method I rely on most. This list collects the methodological papers that taught me how to design and analyze experiments, plus exemplary studies whose research design I want to learn from.

I. Methodology & design

Glenn W. Harrison, John A. List. Field Experiments. Journal of Economic Literature, 42(4):1009–1055, 2004. foundational survey

The taxonomy of "lab in the field" vs. natural vs. framed vs. artefactual field experiments. Cite for any experimental paper.

Esther Duflo, Rachel Glennerster, Michael Kremer. Using Randomization in Development Economics Research: A Toolkit. Handbook of Development Economics, vol. 4, ch. 61, 2007. foundational

Everything you need to run a clean RCT — randomization, attrition, spillovers, ethics.

Donald P. Green, Alan S. Gerber. Field Experiments: Design, Analysis, and Interpretation. W. W. Norton, 2012. foundational

The textbook I recommend to anyone running their first experiment. Practical and statistically careful.

II. Estimation & inference

Joshua D. Angrist, Jörn-Steffen Pischke. Mostly Harmless Econometrics. Princeton University Press, 2009. foundational

Not field-experiment-specific but the language of causal inference that every experimentalist now speaks.

Guido W. Imbens, Donald B. Rubin. Causal Inference for Statistics, Social, and Biomedical Sciences. Cambridge University Press, 2015. foundational

The deeper, more careful companion to Mostly Harmless.

Susan Athey, Guido W. Imbens. The Econometrics of Randomized Experiments. Handbook of Economic Field Experiments, vol. 1, ch. 3, 2017. survey

Modern treatment of estimation, heterogeneous treatment effects, and ML-augmented analysis.

III. Platform & digital experiments

Ron Kohavi, Diane Tang, Ya Xu. Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments: A Practical Guide to A/B Testing. Cambridge University Press, 2020. foundational

How A/B testing actually works at scale; the pitfalls academics rarely discuss but industry knows by heart.

IV. Exemplary studies

Papers I return to as models of how a field experiment should be designed, written up, and interpreted.

Marianne Bertrand, Sendhil Mullainathan. Are Emily and Greg More Employable than Lakisha and Jamal? A Field Experiment on Labor Market Discrimination. American Economic Review, 94(4):991–1013, 2004.

The audit-study design at its sharpest. Read for the careful construction of the experimental variation.

Dean Karlan, John A. List. Does Price Matter in Charitable Giving? Evidence from a Large-Scale Natural Field Experiment. American Economic Review, 97(5):1774–1793, 2007.

A masterclass in scaling a field experiment up — and in writing up null results responsibly.
