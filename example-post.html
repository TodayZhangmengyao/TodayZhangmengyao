Why I pre-register even exploratory experiments

A short defense of pre-registration as a thinking tool, not a credibility signal.

Most discussions of pre-registration frame it as a way to tie your hands — a credible commitment to not p-hack. That framing is correct but incomplete. The bigger benefit, for me, has been that pre-registration forces a kind of thinking I would otherwise skip.

What pre-registration forces

When you write down, in advance, exactly which comparison you'll run, which covariates you'll adjust for, and which subgroup analyses you'll trust — you are forced to confront the question: what would actually convince me?

The discipline of pre-registration is not "I promise not to cheat." It is "I promise to have thought about this before I see the data."

For exploratory experiments — where you genuinely don't know what to expect — that question is harder, not easier. So I pre-register them too, with a section that explicitly labels analyses as exploratory.

What a useful exploratory pre-registration looks like

  • A primary outcome and a primary comparison. Even if you're "just exploring."
  • A list of secondary outcomes, ordered by your prior expectation of effect.
  • A short paragraph describing what pattern would make you update toward a particular mechanism.
  • A note on what you would not conclude from a null result.

That last item is the one I most often forget — and the one that has saved me the most embarrassment.
